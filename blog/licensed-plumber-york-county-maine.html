Why Licensing Matters in Maine — It's Not Just a Formality

Maine requires plumbers to be licensed by the State Board of Licensure for Professional Engineers and Professional Land Surveyors (which also oversees plumber licensing). This isn't just bureaucracy. Here's what a license actually means:

  • Verified training: Licensed plumbers in Maine have completed a multi-year apprenticeship (typically 4 years) under a master plumber, plus classroom instruction and exams
  • Code knowledge: They know Maine's plumbing code, which has specific requirements that differ from other states — especially around freeze protection, well systems, and septic connections
  • Insurance protection: Licensed plumbers carry liability insurance. If an unlicensed worker damages your home, your homeowner's insurance may deny the claim entirely
  • Permit authority: Only licensed plumbers can pull plumbing permits in Maine. Work done without permits can fail home inspections and create legal problems when you sell
  • Accountability: If something goes wrong, you have recourse through the state licensing board. With an unlicensed worker, you're on your own
⚠️ The Real Risk of Unlicensed Work

In York County, we regularly see homeowners who hired a "handyman who does plumbing" to save a few hundred dollars — and ended up paying thousands to have the work redone by a licensed plumber. Unlicensed work can also void your homeowner's insurance coverage for water damage. The savings aren't worth the risk.

How to Verify a Plumber's License in Maine

This is straightforward, and every homeowner should do it before hiring:

  1. Ask the plumber for their license number — any legitimate plumber will give it to you without hesitation
  2. Check it online through the Maine Office of Professional and Occupational Regulation (OPOR) license verification tool at maine.gov
  3. Verify the license type — Maine has Journeyman Plumbers and Master Plumbers. Both can do the work, but only a Master Plumber can pull permits and supervise apprentices
  4. Check that the license is current — Maine plumber licenses must be renewed, and an expired license means the plumber isn't legally authorized to work

If a plumber gets evasive when you ask for their license number, that tells you everything you need to know. Move on.

What to Look for Beyond the License

A license is the minimum. Here's what separates a good plumber from a great one in York County:

  • Local experience: A plumber who works in York County daily knows the quirks — the old galvanized pipes in Biddeford's mill housing, the well water issues in Sanford and Waterboro, the coastal corrosion problems in Kennebunk and York. Local knowledge saves time and prevents mistakes.
  • Insurance: Ask for proof of both liability insurance and workers' compensation. If a plumber is injured on your property without workers' comp, you could be liable.
  • Clear pricing: Good plumbers give you a price before they start — either a flat rate for the job or a clear hourly rate with an estimated timeframe. If they won't commit to a price, find someone who will.
  • Reviews and reputation: Check Google reviews, but also ask neighbors. In towns like Wells, Eliot, South Berwick, and Acton, word of mouth still means something. A plumber with a strong local reputation has earned it.
  • Responsiveness: How quickly do they return your call? A plumber who takes three days to call back for a quote will take longer when something goes wrong on the job.

Questions to Ask Before Hiring

Don't feel awkward asking these. Any professional plumber expects them:

  1. "Are you licensed in Maine, and what's your license number?"
  2. "Do you carry liability insurance and workers' comp?"
  3. "Will you give me a written estimate before starting?"
  4. "Is the estimate a flat rate or hourly? What's included?"
  5. "Will you pull the necessary permits?" (required for most work beyond simple repairs)
  6. "Do you warranty your work? For how long?"
  7. "How long have you been working in the York County area?"
  8. "Can you provide references from recent local jobs?"

A plumber who answers all of these confidently and clearly is someone you can trust.

Red Flags — When to Walk Away

These are non-negotiable warning signs:

  • Won't provide a license number — or gets defensive when you ask
  • No written estimate — "I'll figure it out when I get there" is not acceptable
  • Demands large upfront payment — a reasonable deposit for materials is fine, but paying 100% upfront is a red flag
  • Price is dramatically lower than other quotes — if one quote is $400 and another is $1,200 for the same job, the cheap one is cutting corners or isn't licensed
  • No insurance — this puts your home and your finances at risk
  • Pressure to decide immediately — "this price is only good today" is a sales tactic, not how a professional operates
  • Only accepts cash — legitimate businesses accept checks, cards, or electronic payment and provide receipts
💡 Getting Multiple Quotes

For non-emergency work, get 2–3 quotes. Prices in York County can vary 20–40% between plumbers for the same job. But don't automatically pick the cheapest — compare what's included, the warranty offered, and the plumber's reputation. The middle quote from a well-reviewed, licensed local plumber is almost always the best value.
